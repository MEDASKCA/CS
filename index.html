1. Organisation and Market Selection

1.1 Organisational Context

Smyths Toys Superstores is one of Europe's largest specialist toy retailers, operating over 250 stores across Ireland, the UK, Germany, Austria and Switzerland. Its competitive position is built on broad product assortment, efficient scale purchasing and rapid in-store replenishment (Retail Week, 2022; Smyths Toys, 2023). The company's private ownership structure supports agile decision making, which has historically helped it respond effectively to seasonal demand and supply disruptions.

Smyths' omnichannel model further strengthens its scalability. Real-time stock visibility and click-and-collect are now embedded elements of customer experience, which is increasingly important in digitally mature markets. CITC (2024) identifies that consumers in high-digitisation environments often browse online before purchasing in store, a behaviour that aligns closely with Smyths' operational design. These transferable capabilities form a strong foundation for international expansion when combined with appropriate localisation.

1.2 Strategic Rationale for Entering Saudi Arabia

Saudi Arabia was selected because its market characteristics strongly reinforce Smyths' strengths. Demographically, the country has one of the youngest populations globally, with more than a third under 25 (GASTAT, 2024). This directly aligns with Smyths' core customer base and creates long-term demand for toys, games and children's leisure products. Economically, consumer spending remains resilient; IMF (2024) data indicates stable household expenditure, which is important for a volume-driven retailer.

Retail behaviour strengthens this fit. Shopping malls function as social and leisure destinations, making experiential retail formats more effective. Johnson et al. (2020) notes that in such markets, retailers with broad assortments can differentiate through in-store engagement, an approach that matches Smyths' established format. High digital adoption further supports the case. Saudi consumers often research online before purchasing in store (McKinsey, 2024), and this aligns well with Smyths' strong omnichannel capabilities. While regulatory requirements such as Saudisation and SASO compliance introduce adaptation needs, these are manageable and do not undermine feasibility.

2. Strategic Environmental Analysis

Using a combination of PESTLE, Porter's Five Forces, CAGE, the Industry Life Cycle and Strategic Group Mapping, this section examines the external environment shaping Smyths' entry into Saudi Arabia. Each framework is applied directly to the market rather than described theoretically.

2.1 PESTLE Analysis

Saudi Arabia's regulatory and policy environment provides structural support for international retail. Vision 2030 prioritises foreign investment and consumer-sector diversification, which strengthens market accessibility for international retailers; Porter (1985) highlights that policy support can reduce structural barriers, improving competitive feasibility. Economically, household spending has remained resilient even during global volatility (IMF, 2024), which is essential for a volume-driven toy retailer. Socially, the country's youth demographic remains its strongest opportunity. More than one-third of the population is under 25 (GASTAT, 2024), which significantly exceeds European markets and supports long-term category demand. Technological readiness is also high. CITC (2024) reports strong smartphone penetration and mature digital payments infrastructure, enabling seamless integration of Smyths' omnichannel model. Environmental considerations, including SASO product compliance, require operational adjustments; however, these are routine for multinational retailers and manageable within standard supply processes.

2.2 Porter's Five Forces

Competitive rivalry in Saudi Arabia's toy market is moderate. While hypermarkets such as Carrefour and Lulu stock toys, breadth and depth remain limited compared with specialist retailers. This creates an opportunity for Smyths to position itself as the category destination, consistent with Grewal, Roggeveen and Nordfält's (2017) argument that retailers with wide assortments can capture family-oriented traffic. Supplier power remains moderate; global toy brands maintain strong influence, but Smyths' existing European scale allows it to negotiate effectively, giving it an advantage over local competitors. Buyer power is moderate; although customers are price sensitive, they value product quality and availability. Threat of substitutes, such as digital gaming, is growing; however, Newzoo (2024) reports that Saudi consumers typically purchase both physical and digital entertainment, rather than substituting outright. Threat of new entrants is low because the required assortment, logistics capacity and capital investment create natural barriers.

2.3 CAGE Distance Framework

Cultural and administrative distance between the UK and Saudi Arabia is meaningful but manageable. Consumer preferences in Saudi Arabia emphasise family shopping and in-mall experiences; Johnson et al. (2020) argues that retailers succeed when they adapt formats to reflect local social norms. Administratively, foreign investors must comply with Saudisation and licensing requirements, but these are predictable and widely understood. Geographic distance affects lead times, making local warehousing essential. Economically, Saudi Arabia offers a higher proportion of young, high-spending households than many EU markets, reducing economic distance in terms of demand profile.

2.4 Industry Life Cycle

Saudi Arabia's toy and family entertainment retail sector sits between the growth and early-maturity stages. Euromonitor (2024) projects continued expansion driven by youth population growth and increased mall development. The sector is not yet saturated, and specialist toy retail is comparatively under-developed compared with Europe. This creates a timing advantage; Grant (2019) notes that entering during a growth phase allows firms to capture market share before competitive structures solidify.

2.5 Strategic Group Mapping

Competitive analysis indicates two main strategic groups in Saudi Arabia: hypermarkets offering broad but shallow assortments; and small independent toy stores with narrow ranges and limited scale. There is no large, mid-priced, full-range specialist similar to Smyths. This strategic gap positions Smyths to occupy an uncontested space: high-assortment, value-based specialist retail. This conclusion aligns with Whittington et al. (2019), who argue that identifying under-served strategic positions can support defensible advantage in new markets.

3. Strategy Formulation and Market Entry Strategy

Smyths' competitive strategy for Saudi Arabia builds directly on the opportunity patterns identified in Section 2. Instead of applying frameworks in isolation, this section integrates Porter's Generic Strategies with retailer-specific considerations such as assortment leadership, omnichannel expectations and localisation requirements.

3.1 Strategic Positioning Using Porter's Generic Strategies

The most viable strategic posture for Smyths in Saudi Arabia is cost-leadership blended with differentiation through assortment breadth. The toy market exhibits high price sensitivity; however, customers also strongly value product availability and choice (Euromonitor, 2024). This dual priority means a pure cost-leadership or pure differentiation approach would be insufficient. What strengthens Smyths' competitiveness is its capability to achieve lower unit costs through global buying power while still offering a wider range than local rivals.

Porter (1980) notes that competitive advantage is strongest when firms align strategy with industry structure. In KSA, where hypermarkets dominate low-priced general merchandise and independent stores lack scale, Smyths can take the middle ground: value-driven pricing supported by operational efficiency, complemented by broad category depth. This positioning fits with Whittington et al. (2019), who emphasise that sustained advantage often emerges from combining efficiency with distinctive offerings.

3.2 Local Value Chain Configuration

The value chain in Saudi Arabia requires selective localisation rather than full redesign. Several components need to be adapted:

Inbound logistics: importing via Jeddah or Dammam requires extended lead times; therefore, local warehousing is essential. Lean logistics principles from Slack et al. (2022) support configuring replenishment cycles to reduce stockouts.

Store operations: mall-based stores must emphasise experience. Johnson et al. (2020) highlights the importance of adapting formats to social shopping cultures.

Marketing and customer engagement: digital-first campaigns work well in Saudi Arabia's highly connected population; McKinsey (2024) notes that KSA consumers use mobile channels extensively during purchase decisions.

These modifications ensure Smyths retains its scale-based advantages while meeting expectations of the Saudi retail environment.

3.3 Market Entry Mode

The recommended market entry mode is a fully owned subsidiary, supported by local partnerships for regulatory compliance and mall negotiations. A partnership-only model would limit operational control; Johanson and Vahlne (2009) argue that firms benefit from increasing commitment when markets demonstrate long-term potential. Given Saudi Arabia's promising demographics and stable demand, full ownership allows Smyths to keep control of merchandising, pricing and brand identity. Local advisors will still be required to manage Saudi licensing, labour compliance and SASO certification.

3.4 Implementation Roadmap

A phased rollout reduces risk while allowing controlled learning:

Phase 1: Secure legal licensing, recruit Saudi-national store teams and establish regional warehousing.

Phase 2: Open two flagship stores in high-traffic malls (Riyadh, Jeddah) supported by a full omnichannel launch.

Phase 3: Extend to secondary cities, supported by stronger local brand recognition.

This roadmap aligns with Grant's (2019) recommendation that international expansion should balance commitment with staged learning.

4. Performance Measurement and Strategic Control

4.1 Balanced Scorecard for Smyths' Saudi Market Entry

To ensure Smyths' expansion into Saudi Arabia remains strategically aligned, measurable and adaptive, a Balanced Scorecard (BSC) provides a structured mechanism for monitoring early-stage performance. The BSC translates strategic intent into practical indicators that reflect both operational execution and long-term capability building. As Kaplan and Norton (1996) emphasise, balanced metrics allow firms to detect whether early activities are creating the foundations for competitive advantage rather than relying solely on financial lag indicators.

Financial Perspective Early financial measures must reflect the realities of market entry, where initial costs are high and brand establishment is ongoing. Grant (2019) notes that focusing on contribution margins and cost-to-serve is more meaningful than overall profitability in new-market contexts. For Smyths, key metrics include gross margin %, revenue per square foot and early logistics cost ratios. These indicators provide insight into whether the localisation of warehousing and assortment strategy is supporting sustainable unit economics.

Customer Perspective Customer acquisition and experience matter significantly in Saudi retail environments, where mall-based shopping dominates and digital engagement influences decision-making (McKinsey, 2024). Accordingly, customer KPIs include Net Promoter Score, product availability percentage, click-and-collect usage and digital engagement rates. These help assess whether Smyths' omnichannel model is gaining traction and whether local consumers perceive value in the breadth of the assortment.

Internal Process Perspective Local operational readiness determines whether the customer promise can be reliably delivered. Slack et al. (2022) emphasise that replenishment speed and execution consistency are critical differentiators in retail. Key internal metrics include replenishment cycle time, planogram compliance, waste and shrinkage levels and whether SASO product certification processes are meeting required timelines. Strong performance here indicates whether the adapted Saudi value chain is functioning as intended.

Learning and Growth Perspective Because Saudisation and cultural adaptation are central to the country's labour landscape, Smyths must invest in its workforce as part of capability building. KPIs include Saudisation compliance %, staff training hours per employee, managerial localisation progress and retention rates among newly recruited Saudi staff. Johnson et al. (2020) highlight that successful internationalisation requires deepening internal understanding of local customer norms, which makes capability-building metrics strategically essential.

4.2 Strategic Control Mechanisms

In a new market characterised by regulatory complexity and strong social norms, strategic control mechanisms help ensure that early deviations are detected and corrected. Regular KPI dashboards, monthly store performance reviews and local compliance audits support operational control. Whittington et al. (2019) note that feedback loops between strategy and performance are most effective when indicators are simple, timely and aligned with decision rights. For Smyths, quarterly strategic reviews should assess whether store-level learning justifies adjustments to assortment depth, pricing or store format. In addition, the company should integrate insights from customer data and digitally tracked behaviour into operational improvements.

4.3 Additional Control Insights

Managing early-stage performance in the Saudi market requires close attention to the parts of execution that tend to create the most risk. The first is regulatory compliance. Licensing, Saudisation requirements and SASO product approvals can cause delays if not monitored proactively. Hill and Hult (2020) explain that firms entering new administrative environments benefit from short, regular review cycles because they prevent small compliance issues from accumulating into larger operational disruptions. Applying this lesson, Smyths would be better served by a four-week regulatory checkpoint rather than relying on annual audits.

A second control priority is ensuring that customer data directly informs operational decisions. Saudi consumers rely heavily on mobile research and in-mall browsing; this means that differences between expected and actual demand can emerge quickly. Slack et al. (2022) highlight that retailers create more stable operations when digitally collected customer signals are tied to replenishment, space allocation and product availability rather than remaining as stand-alone reporting. For Smyths, weekly reviews of availability levels, click-and-collect patterns and footfall-related demand variations would provide the evidence needed to adjust store routines before issues become visible to customers.

The final control area involves workforce capability development. Because Saudisation shapes hiring requirements, Smyths cannot depend entirely on expatriate experience. Johnson et al. (2020) note that capability-building needs to begin early in international expansion because cultural understanding, role clarity and service expectations take time to stabilise. Monitoring indicators such as staff retention, completion of training programmes and progress towards Saudi-national supervisory roles helps ensure that capability building keeps pace with store rollout.

Together, these control mechanisms support a more adaptive form of performance management. They encourage shorter feedback loops, reduce avoidable operational risks and strengthen the connection between strategy and frontline learning. Grant (2019) emphasises that international retail strategies succeed when firms treat the early entry period as a learning phase rather than a replication exercise, and these controls support that approach.

5. Critical Framework Evaluation

Throughout this project, I found that the frameworks only became meaningful when I stopped treating them as separate exercises and instead used them to interrogate the same problem from different angles. Each tool revealed something useful, but each also produced blind spots that I needed to compensate for. This section reflects on how the frameworks genuinely contributed to my judgement about Smyths' expansion into Saudi Arabia and where their limitations became obvious once the analysis moved from theory into operational implications.

5.1 PESTLE: Useful Direction, Limited Prioritisation

PESTLE initially helped me understand the broad landscape, but its value increased only after I ranked the factors by their real operational weight. For example, the youth demographic and digital readiness were clearly central advantages for Smyths. In contrast, environmental factors had far less influence compared with mandatory Saudisation or SASO compliance. Grant (2019) argues that environmental scans require prioritisation, which matched my experience. Without ranking, PESTLE risks producing a long list that treats everything as equally important. Once I reweighted the categories, the model became more practical. It clarified that the political, economic and social dimensions would shape 80 percent of the early decisions, while the others required monitoring rather than strategic reorientation.

5.2 Porter's Five Forces: Clear Structure but Overly Static

Porter's Five Forces helped me assess how the toy retail structure differs from Europe, particularly around rivalry and substitutes. However, I also saw how rigid the model can be. In Saudi Arabia, the boundaries between toys, digital entertainment and lifestyle products are blurred, yet Porter's framework still forces the analyst to fit everything into fixed boxes. Whittington et al. (2019) notes that modern retail competition often crosses category lines, and I found this to be true. Substitutes, for example, were not just other toys but also digital gaming and interactive entertainment. The model became more powerful when I used market data from Euromonitor (2024) and Newzoo (2024) to quantify how Saudi families actually spend. Once I adjusted for this, Five Forces helped me see that the real competitive threat was not hypermarkets but the broadening of entertainment choices. The model was helpful for structure but required reinterpretation to reflect how customers behave rather than how the model expects industries to behave.

5.3 CAGE: Exposed Distance but Needed Translation into Operations

CAGE was most useful in making me slow down and think about the forms of distance that are easy to underestimate. Cultural distance stood out most clearly. Saudi Arabia's family shopping culture immediately signalled that store format and service design cannot simply be copied from Europe. Johnson et al. (2020) argues that successful market entry depends on understanding local norms, and CAGE forced me to confront that early. However, CAGE also remained quite high level. For example, knowing that administrative distance exists did not tell me how fast SASO certification typically moves or what this means for lead times. I found that the framework acted more as a diagnostic starting point, and the real insight came when I translated each distance into specific operational decisions, such as investing in local warehousing to absorb geographic delay or hiring Saudi staff early to embed cultural competence.

5.4 Industry Life Cycle: Strong on Timing, Weak on Category Variation

The life cycle model helped confirm that the Saudi toy retail market sits between growth and early maturity, which supported the strategic timing of entry. However, its weakness became clear once I analysed the category at a more granular level. The toy market is not one cycle, but many. Outdoor toys behave differently from educational products, and collectibles behave differently from baby items. Grant (2019) highlights that sub-market mapping produces more reliable guidance than treating the entire sector as a single curve. Once I applied this logic, I noticed that Smyths' advantage would be uneven across categories. The model still provided valuable directional insight, but it needed breaking down into multiple curves before it influenced assortment strategy in a meaningful way.

5.5 Strategic Group Mapping: Most Immediately Actionable but Narrow on Experience Factors

Of all the models, Strategic Group Mapping was the most directly useful because it showed a clear gap in the market. No retailer currently occupies the wide-assortment, mid-price specialist segment in Saudi Arabia. This gave me a concrete positioning opportunity. However, the framework focuses mainly on observable attributes like price and range. Grewal et al. (2017) argue that modern retail competition increasingly rests on customer experience, something the model does not naturally capture. I had to overlay experiential variables such as store interactivity and omnichannel reliability. Once I did that, the map made more sense, but this manual adjustment highlighted the model's structural limitations.

5.6 Integrating the Frameworks: What Actually Shaped My Judgment

What became clear through this evaluation is that each framework revealed something credible, but none worked well in isolation. PESTLE signalled the enabling conditions. Five Forces pushed me to consider the structure behind consumer behaviour. CAGE forced attention onto adaptation rather than assumption. The industry life cycle grounded the timing of entry. Strategic Group Mapping provided a practical, market-level opening.

Together, these tools gave me a balanced view of what Smyths can realistically achieve in Saudi Arabia and where early risks sit. More importantly, they helped me translate theoretical tools into operational decisions, such as:

  • prioritising store location within malls because of cultural shopping norms;
  • investing early in local compliance to reduce administrative distance;
  • calibrating assortment depth according to sub-market life cycles;
  • and shaping positioning based on a genuine gap in the market rather than an assumed one.
